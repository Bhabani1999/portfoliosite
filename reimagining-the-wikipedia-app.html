Reimagining the Wikipedia app experience

Read and discover articles in a better way with the redesigned Wikipedia app.

Wikipedia, since 2000, has been the panacea to all information inadequacies. With the immense volume of articles spanning across multiple languages of the world, it finds use from simple fact lookup on a film-icon to intense, in-depth research for a school thesis.

Users access Wikipedia through the Wikipedia web or the native mobile application on Android and IOS platforms. While the Wikipedia web continues to see striking footfalls, the mobile app’s usage has more or less been stagnating with time.

01

The Problem

Users have separate motivations for using the web and mobile version of Wikipedia.
The existing mobile app tries to address them by changing the structure of Wikipedia on the mobile interface.

The results are disheveling for regular users of Wikipedia. They find a completely different Wikipedia experience on the app as well as difficulty in the discovery of articles and poor reading experience.

The Solution

I reimagined the Wikipedia app, by trying to unify the Wikipedia web and mobile experience and solving the pain points faced during the app usage. The redesign helps users to discover articles of their interest quickly and eases their intrinsic information needs. With the app, users can have a focused reading experience with easy navigation in and between sections.

01

REDESIGNED LANDING PAGE

Get right into action

By clearly segmenting the two actions, the new app gives the users a sense of direction.  They can choose to carry on their suspended research or dive deep into the rabbit hole of Wikipedia.

The grid-like view allows users to choose a quick action to start reading from their favourite topics.

02

TODAY’S WIKI & PORTALS

Instantly access Wikipedia’s best articles

Today’s wiki, a daily curated box of articles ranging from ‘Did you know’ to ’What happened to this day’. It gives Wikipedians a peek into the past and  chance to gain new knowledge.

The redesign makes it easy to access ‘Today’s wiki’ from any date while providing all the content in an easy-to-intake and consistent manner much like the WIkipedia web.

Portals serve as main pages of specific broad subjects. They allow users to access their articles of interest or develop their knowledge of a particular subject right from the start. The redesign allows users to access all the portals from a single screen.

03

IMPROVED SEARCH EXPERIENCE

Easily find what you are looking for

Wikipedia sees most of its traffic from searches. A separate dedicated page for search allows users to quickly spot what other Wikipedians are searching for and what is in the news.

Search across multiple languages is fast and intuitive in the redesign. Classification of search results, like in the Wikipedia web, helps users with different information needs to identify the right results for them.

04

TODAY’S WIKI & PORTALS

Easily navigate between and keep track of open articles

Wikipedia sees extensive navigation from a single article’s core to its peripheral articles solely through hyperlinks.

The new app helps users to keep track of the hierarchy of their open articles and switch between them without any fuss. So if you started from ‘Davinci’, you can always know how you reached ‘Persistence of Memory.’
Wikipedians can also create a folder of articles inside which they can research on a particular topic without any distraction.

Research

Why I chose to redesign Wikipedia?

I have been a Wikipedia fan right from when I would scourge the site for information on my school projects. For over a decade, I have been gaining knowledge on vast subjects as well as being a part of the editing community. I have understood why Wikipedia continues to grow even without any visible design system or good software architecture. The mobile app, launched late, elevated its power by enabling users to have a storehouse of information right in their pockets. The mobile app, however, had a lot to deliver in terms of architecture and design.

Being its regular user, I could see the pain points and the inadequacies. Therefore I took it upon myself to reimagine the mobile app.

Figuring out the flow

To improve the overall experience, features had to be placed where the users expected them. I tried to re-enact the information architecture based on insights from user research.

Some major changes include:

  • Most searches were keywords that came up in media conversations or trending news. It made sense to reinforce the search experience by listing the above features alongside.
  • Majority percentage of users according to user research opened the app for intrinsic learning. Arming the home screen with correct app features can help users to get started with their intention as quickly as possible.
  • A significant number of users opened the app to check up on their edits. A separate page for keeping track of edits would help this section of users in completing their actions quick.

Studying the existing app: Explore Page

The explore page had very little to explore.....

While the landing screen was named ‘Explore’ there was little in terms of finding something to get started with reading.

The explore page was an infinite chronological feed of Wiki’s main page.

The randomiser, which offered random articles to start with, was hidden deep down the scroll.

The ‘Today’s main page’ button does nothing but direct to the explore page again.

The content gets repeated in multiple languages which makes no sense to a user who is opening Wikipedia for intrinsic learning.

All in all, there is little direction for a user looking for gaining knowledge, the reason most users open the app according to user research, on the starting screen of the app.

Pagination vs infinite scroll

While infinite scroll surely makes the user stay longer on an app, it is not always a one-size-fits-all solution for an app. Infinite scroll is necessary when users are not looking for something specific. In such cases, they require a lot of content to find something to get invested. On the other hand, I felt pagination becomes relevant when users are making a decision. It gives users a sense of control and direction.
In Wikipedia, when users land on the explore tab, they do so with the sole aim to find a good article that can further their knowledge. Talking to users I realised, showering them with an infinite feed (a feed of chronological wiki’s main pages) only ends up confusing them and forcing them to close the app in frustration. Pagination or dividing the content into chunks appears a better option

Improving discovery of new articles

When users open Wikipedia, they do so with the following objectives.

  • To continue reading articles, they had left off or to find topics to start reading for intrinsic learning.
  • To search for a fact-lookup or a trending topic.

It made sense to redesign the explore and search screen that helped users achieve the above goals.

Studying the existing app:
Navigating between open articles

Navigation between articles is difficult in existing app

The implementation of viewing and managing open tabs or articles in the current Wikipedia app is not suited to the reason users use the feature.

Common behavioural patterns while browsing Wikipedia include frequent to-and-fro between subtopics and parent topics to keep track of information input, exploring subtopics (numbering hundreds during research ) through hyperlinks and other practices that mimic searching of information in an encyclopaedia. The current Wikipedia’s tab functionality is full of friction for its users. Implementation of viewing and managing open tabs or articles in the current Wikipedia app is not suited to the reason users use the feature.

In a desktop browser, this friction is comparably small because of the large screen estate and visibility of all open tabs. In the app, flicking through tabs is cumbersome and hinders information lookup.

Deciphering how users browsed articles: The story of John

John, a regular Wikipedia user and a DC megafan, starts reading about the new Batman film. He clicks on the Batman v Superman link and then goes on to learn about Zack Snyder and ultimately reads about Reebok through hyperlinks.
Now he wants to come back to complete the rest of ’The Batman’ article. He presses the back button a few times and finds himself there. After some time he starts another chain of articles and reads about ‘Suicide squad’ which is a Zack Snyder directorial. Now he is intrigued by Zack Snyder and wants to continue reading his article.
However, he can’t find the article as pressing the back button remitted it earlier. He is frustrated. Now he can do two things: Start a new search on Zack Synder or find the same chain that made him land him on Zack Synder.
Finding the same chain is always hard and starting a new search again will deviate him from his primary purpose: Read about ‘The Batman’.

The friction John faces is even more prominent when a user carries out intense research on a particular topic. The current way of achieving this functionality had to be revamped.

Facilitating easy navigation between tabs functionality

Users primarily had three needs while performing the above action.

  • Be able to track the trail of articles opened through hyperlinks.
  • Not get distracted from the parent article by the chain of subtopics.
  • Come back to continue the research whenever needed.

I tried to allow the users to achieve the above goals in the redesigned Wikipedia by changing the way tabs function.

Studying the existing app:
The Reading Experience

It is difficult for users to retrieve information

The article pages had no evident cohesion or consistency in design. Different functionalities were scattered all across the page with no proper structure or direction to reach them.

Tables and pictures broke the flow in long article pages.

The option to shift to ‘Talk page’ and ‘Edit History page’ was found at the bottom of the page.

All in all, reading articles without distraction was a painful experience.

Simplifying the article pages

While on an article users performed the following actions:

  • Collect information from the opened article and interact with different components.
  • Navigate through sections of the article.
  • Check ’Talk’ and ‘Read History’ page for additional background information on the article.
  • Navigate between branches of opened articles.
  • Change language, bookmark and similar other functionalities.

Initially I explored the many different ways a user could implement the actions. I also explored different layouts to perfect the article page.

Helping users conduct research properly

A significant amount of time is spent by users in looking up references and making sense of every information in an article. Good research is also incomplete without an adequate amount of notes and information on peripheral topics.

The redesigned Wikipedia provides a better reading experience by allowing users a quick overview of every hyperlink.

At the same time taking notes is quick and easy in the new Wikipedia app. Users can save anecdotes, reference, sections and even entire articles to aid their reading of a topic.

Exploring the visual identity

As a personal goal, I wanted to stretch my UI skills and explore different art directions for the Wikipedia app. To achieve this, I decided to understand how people perceived Wikipedia as a brand and What wikipedia represented to them.

For most people, Wikipedia connects them to the past. It is a bridge connecting them to the information of yesteryears. At the same time, it aims to bring on the utopian concept of World Brain where knowledge and ideas are received, sorted, summarised, digested, clarified and compared all digitally.

In simpler words, Wikipedia serves as the connection between the ancient and the modern.

Additionally, Wikipedia calls for inclusivity. The openness to other languages inspires to preserve cultural diversity despite globalisation. The sole purpose of Wikipedia, as claimed by its founders, is to make information accessible to everyone.

I designed my visual style to preserve the said values of Wikipedia.

Fonts & Typography
Primary Typeface
Circular Std Book/Medium/Bold
Secondary Typeface
FreightText Book/Medium/Bold

I chose my primary typeface to be a Geometric Grotesk. It helped reinforce the principles of Wikipedia in its visual identity: Create a modern world in which every single person on the planet is given free access to the sum of all human knowledge.

The secondary typeface is a Serif font face. It is used as outlines throughout article pages. Its visual style stands as a symbol of the centuries of ancient information that is accessible to everyone through Wiki.

The contrast in Primary and Secondary typefaces also helped reinforce information hierarchy and spatial consistency.

Color

My choice of colours was stripped to the very basics: black and white, representing Wikipedia’s stand on oneness.
The only other colour that I chose was a blue hue (Primary Color), which is symbolic of the World Wide Web and how Wikipedia has changed the realm of information discovery through the Internet.

Additionally, reducing hues helped to reduce distractions and help users to easily read through articles.

End

Staring back and Pushing Forward

This was a fun personal project to work. It involved a lot of research and iterations which I thoroughly enjoyed. It was also the first time I conceived UI design within a set of constraints which I think brought out the best in me.

Choosing what not to do and sticking to deadline

Throughout the project, I had several ideas that I thought could be implemented. After a time, it became essential to only move forward with only those that could be categorised under my pre-set design goals.
During this project, I learnt that sticking to the goals and design principles help bring out a better product overall. While tons of features might seem attractive upfront, implementing them without being considerate of the vision brings out a half-baked product.

Understanding when not to redesign products

I have always wondered why web entities such as Amazon, Reddit or Wikipedia have not done a design overhaul after being so much of years in existence.
Understanding Why Wikipedia has stuck to its classic design of the 2000s helped me to understand why companies are reluctant to change about how users interact with their products.
