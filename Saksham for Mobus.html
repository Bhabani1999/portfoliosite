Saksham
for Mobus

Using inclusive design to create a better travel experience for Mobus’s users.

In 2018, the Govt of Odisha rolled out city-wide bus services. The services could be accessed through a mobile application to automate transport experience.

One year later, the app’s poor user experience and inadequate understanding of its core set of users had most passengers stay away from the app altogether and stressful travel experience for those who used it.

01

The Problem

A Look Into The Numbers

90%

of total disabled population use Mobus as their only mode of transport.

19000

or 12% of total city population are reported as persons with disabilitites of movement.

46%

of daily commuters on Mobus are aged above 40.

In sharp contrast to these numbers, the existing app doesn’t understand the problems people with disabilities of movement and senior citizens face in daily transit.

“ I  plan every simple trip to the city corner one-two days before. Plan gets done; I wait for the bus on the day of travel only to find out the bus has no accessibility and is swamped to the core. By some means, I get on to the bus, complete my journey full of stress, board off only to find out the lift does not work at the station”.

Surya Prakash recounts his daily struggles.

Unpredictability is a significant barrier for those who want to live an active lifestyle with special needs.

02

The
Opportunity

How might we reduce friction in daily transit on Mobus by enabling access and visibility of information, so people with special needs can also travel anxiety-free and enjoy the journey?

03

The
Solution

“Everything we do is on the web. So, if all of these amenities are not available to a particular portion of people just because they are not like the person building the app, then that is mean and selfish.”

-An advocate of accessibility

SAKSHAM by Mobus

Motivated by the aim to alleviate this nonuniformity, I designed ‘Saksham by Mobus.’
Saksham means being able and independent in Odia.

Saksham allows people with disabilities of movement and senior citizens, a core part of Mobus’s users, to have a better booking and travel experience on buses.
Saksham, though, has been designed for a particular section of people, any general population can easily be a part of the experience.

04

Research

Picking up the pieces

Completing the research gave me a boatload of insights and understanding of the users. I summarized the findings and prioritized some ideas upon which I would work to create a solution.

INSIGHT #1

No information on accessibility

Users have no way of knowing whether the buses they will travel in will be accessible or have ramps or the stations they will go to will have accessible toilets.

INSIGHT #3

Unpredicatbility in getting a bus

Even if users pre-book a ticket, the buses’ wheelchair spaces would already be occupied at times. While all passengers are interested in knowing when a bus will come or how full it will be, this information is particularly important for passengers with disabilities.

INSIGHT #5

Missing clarity on the degree of accessibility

“ Many forms of transport say they are wheelchair accessible, but they don’t have any means to assure me that my large power wheelchair is included in their definition of wheelchairs.”

INSIGHT #7

No alternative routes in bus app

Unlike most users, travel time is not the only factor in deciding the route between two places. Persons with disabilities have to choose between accessibility, accessible roads between stops to the final destination, availability of wheelchair seats, and finally, time. The bus app alienates them by offering no alternative routes.

INSIGHT #9

Bus services’ indifference to users’ needs

“ The service owners have to do more than just putting in a few accessible signs at buses and stops. They have to do a lot more for us to see the end of this indifference”.

INSIGHT #2

Hesitant of traveling in buses

Due to the services not facilitating the needs of persons on wheelchairs, many people are usually hesitant to venture out as they have to face many hurdles. Others have developed a negative perception of the bus services and have accepted that they are going to suffer anyway.

INSIGHT #4

Anxiety and stress during travel

Almost all users reported feeling stressed during their travel. Not knowing the exact stop to get off or the time left to reach the destination doesn’t help. Added to them are the woes of not being able to board on and off the buses easily. The bus doesn’t provide help either. Most of its purpose is completed after booking the ticket

INSIGHT #6

Lack of a travel companion

“ Being a person with special needs, I continuously require the help of people while traveling. While people usually are helpful, at times, I wish technology could help me in certain situations.

INSIGHT #8

Uncooperative bus conductors

Bus conductors rarely offer help to the users in boarding and off. With no system to monitor their behaviors, they enjoy unwanted freedom and use up a chunk of the resources.

INSIGHT #10

Technology aversion

Elderly users have a negative perception of technology’s problem-solving capabilities and often stay away from using utility apps.

“ I cannot figure out the necessary tasks on the app. Unless it is  fixed, I will always get scared when I think of using it. “

05

Reframing
the
problem

Unpredictability, lack of useful information and incomplete system feedback led to users having a stressful travel time in buses.

Talking to users and studying their opinions led me to realize unpredictability in having access to buses is not the only major problem. In their view, this nonfuniformity exists due to prevailing ignorance of their needs and their rights to live a life like every human being.

The discomfort and uncertainty they feel during travel due to no system feedback is a greater problem for them. This happens mostly due to no information on when to board off, lack of information on accessibility and missing data which might be unimportant for the non-disabled population but a life-saver for people on wheelchairs,  

Bothways, the goal of being able to enjoy traveling in the city is not achieved.

Designing
Saksham

IT STARTED WITH MY LOVE FOR THE CITY.

BHUBANESWAR: THE CITY OF EVERY MAN JACK

Bhubaneswar has always been the city for everyone. It has grown from being a mountainous heap of sandstone to being the smartest city in India. Part of the reason being the City’s stand on inclusivity. Everyone has access to sustainable livelihoods and affordable essential services.

Traveling through the city without any barrier is always an empowering experience.
Growing up in this ambitious city, it pained me to see people on wheelchairs and older people having a hard time using the Bus services.I wanted to make sure inclusivity in transport did not just rest in words.

Figuring out the flow

Building user flows early on enabled me to design each screen keeping in mind its position in the task flow. Studying all possible scenarios helps to rule out mistakes in the designs later-on.

The Landing Screen

The landing screen being the first interaction between the app and its users was designed to provide maximum utility in different use cases at the same time yet be simple to aid in quick decision making.

Users can see the stations around them, use favorite routes, and find travel routes. They can navigate to ‘stations’ , ‘trips’ , ‘notifications’ and access their profile all from the landing page.

06

Information on every bus & stop

Users can see detailed information on individual parts of their entire trip (start time, distance & duration). Users can track the buses and add customized alerts by going through the bus schedule.

Visualising the bus before riding

Users can see the availability of wheelchair seats inside buses. Details on-ramps, entrances, mats, and similar assets can allow users to decide on the compatibility of buses. Users can better choose by going through user-generated videos of bus entrances and interiors.

07

Never miss a trip

Getting alerts directly to the phone allows users to have plenty of time to prepare for travel and visualise their journey. This helps people with wheelchairs negate potential delays on roads.

The countdown timer inside the app reveals the virtual companion that will guide users during travel.

08

Never forget a thing

The trip checklist reminds users not to miss important things while going out for travel. After checking off the list, users can start their journey and getting guided by the virtual companion.

Users can add a checklist right after booking a ticket or add them later.

09

The Virtual Companion

It can be daunting for newer travelers (especially for people with wheelchairs) of a given route to keep track of unfamiliar stops in an unknown location. With the proposed virtual companion feature, users will be able to watch a live view of their position along the route on a map, with indications provided for remaining time and number of stops left on their journey.

This allows the user to feel more confident in their movement around the city by providing a sense of “hand-holding” in the form of live visual feedback. It provides a distraction-free modal with all the utilities required to travel, a click away. Additional app integrations like Spotify and Uber help ease pain points and simplifies tasks.

10

Alerts on malfunctioning ramps

With crowdsources reports, users will be notified when there is a malfunctioning ramp or lift in the route of their scheduled trips.

They can view the exact nature of the report and can even contact the reporter.

11

Report Malfunctioning Ramps

Users can themselves create reports when they similarly encounter any inaccesibe places, broken ramps  etc., while traveling.
This information gets relayed to every other user.

This creates a close-knit community of users where everybody is helping each other through crowdsourced data.

12

Request assistance any time during travel

Since, the conductors and drivers already use their end of the app to scan tickets and monitor boardings and exits, integrating all the stakeholders into the ecosystem can help reduce information gaps.

Users can request assistance from conductors and bus helpers. Conductors will receive notifications on request for assistance. This is useful when buses are overcrowded and it becomes difficult for conductors to offer help to passengers individually. At the same time, a review system can help monitor their conduct.

With ample knowledge of users and their needs, I brainstormed ideas on where to improve the experience and found out the opportunities. While I had several ideas, I prioritized a select few to convert them into working solutions.

OPPORTUNITY #1

Including accesibility signs

Indicating which buses and stops are accessible can help users to preplan their trips.  Every detail that is important for wheelchair users to navigate the city should be introduced at every app flow.

OPPORTUNITY #3

Virtual Companion

It is often scary for users to estimate when to board off because unlike people with no diabilities , wheelchair users use up more time than them. Any sort of feedback modal that relays out all details can be pacifying for users.

OPPORTUNITY #5

Offering multiple routes

Users can have mutiple routes from A to B. It will allow them to choose between routes depending on the type of accesibilities available

OPPORTUNITY #7

Booking tickets for friends and vice-versa

Most of the time, users book tickets for their friends when they travel together and share the tickets through WhatsApp. Allowing users to directly send the trip to the app at a friend’s end can reduce unnecessary effort.

OPPORTUNITY #9

Way to report malfunctioning accessibilities

Getting to know malfunctioning ramps, lifts and similar accesibilities on the route of users’ scheduled trip can allow them to plan out or change the trip

OPPORTUNITY #2

Better booking flow

The curret flow is outdated and doesnt serve all cases. A new flow is required which can be perceived even by technology-averse people.

OPPORTUNITY #4

Making conductors responsible

A way to be able to ask conductors for help by notifying on the interface on their side. Users can also rate their conduct. These ratings can then be used to monitor coductors.

OPPORTUNITY #6

Enabling booking from curret location.

Current route searches allow searches only between two stops. Users have to use additional apps such as Google maps to reach the desired stop. Unlike people with no disabilities, wheelchair users can’t afford the same. They can only choose a route if the road to the boarding stop is accessible, which is not so in many cases.

OPPORTUNITY #8

Timely Alerts

Users tend to forget about their upcoming trips. The app can notify users at the required time. This can make the app feel more humane.

OPPORTUNITY #10

Sharing location to contacts

Wheelchair users often feel the need to notify their location to relieve their family and friends’ worries. They can also need this feature for emergencies.

Design Principles

A strong foundation and guiding principles enabled me to make correct design decisions and create a consistent user experience across the app.

Familiar

The flows in the app should correspond to the mental models of the users. They should be similar to how users go about in real life.

Simple

With a significant set of users agreeing to be technologically averse, it was essential to design every component that marks the aim of tasks without complicating.

Inclusive

While the users would be majorly wheelchair users, an inclusive design meant solving for one and extending to many. The app had to be so that even people with no disabilities can find their way through the app.

Wireframes

I started with pen and paper and jotted out ideas on paper before I moved to design digitally. It helped to put my thoughts on forth quickly. As I advanced more, I restricted myself to creating on design software, which enabled me to maintain consistency across screens.

The Thumb zone

A hurdle faced during design was that most wheelchair users could only afford to use touch-screens with only one hand, as the other hand would be occupied in navigating the wheelchair. Since most use cases of the app would be on the road, it was necessary to design strictly such that most essential tasks could be done with a single hand quickly. Repeated iterations helped in overcoming this hurdle.

User Testing

Validating every decision through constant feedback across the process helped find a reason behind them. User testing allowed me to reduce my assumptions in the visual design of app components and made the app more user-friendly.

Microcopy

The flows in the app were so made to correspond to the mental models of the users. They were modified to how users go about in real life.

Labels

With a significant set of users agreeing to be technologically averse, it was essential to design every component that cleary labels every major task , input field , etc. Appropriate changes were made after validation

Hierarchy

Adjusted information architecture to allow users to take in information quickly without cognitive overloading.
Search routes were more defined in hierarchy after testing to allow users to compare several results quickly.

End

Staring back and Pushing Forward

I realized the words of Disability rights activist Elise Ray “When we design for disability first, you often stumble upon solutions that are better than those when we design for the norm." Designing within limitations helped me to look from lateral viewpoints and find out simpler solutions.

Interviewing people with wheelchairs and learning about their struggles opened my eyes to problems that we have learned to ignore over time. Designing with empathy was never better realised.

Working consistently over a month was a helpful constraint that enabled me to work smart and quickly. In the future, I wish to explore how this product actually stands in the real-world. There were few areas Iike creating an incentivized environment for creating reports and initial ideas like creating a social connected ecosystem where selected contacts can see where you are travelling, that I could not explore due to time constraints. I will try to gain more feedback from people with wheelchairs and observe how they make use of the product to improve upon it.
