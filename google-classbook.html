01

The
Brief

At the beginning of each new semester or school year, teachers are faced with the challenge of remembering names for a large number of new students. Design an experience to help an educator match faces to names, with the goal of shortening the time needed to reach complete un-aided accuracy.

Um, Why do teachers try to remember names of their students?

  • Calling students by names helps them feel recognised as individuals and darws out shy individuals into discusiion.
  • It has been observed that by identifying students with their names makes the subject matter more accesssible.
  • Also by doing so, teachers communicate respect and help make the class more interactive.

Most teachers admit the importance of remembering the names of their students. They consciously put efforts unless the class is too large. The reasons teachers believe the practice helps are that it helps in making the class closely knit and it initiates discussions between them and students as well as between fellow students.

Sure there are techniques that teachers apply which don’t need an app...

It was essential to understand the shortcomings of the current methods, if there were any, before considering to augment them with technology. Studying the current methods was an attempt to identify the pain points and places where technology can be introduced to help teachers.

Students writing down unique hobbies and characteristics on postcards for teachers to form associations.

PAIN POINTS

In a huge class, maintenace of cards is difficult. Teachers rarely go through all the cards after the first time. Remembering all the associations is another challenge altogether.

OPPORTUNITIES

Association through unique characteristics is a proven technique for memorisation. Data collection and management can be simplified for teachers through apps. Practicing the cards helps to remember the associations for long.

Asking students to mention their names in every interaction.

PAIN POINTS

Students feel the teacher cannot remember their names. Actually matching faces to names takes a long time which is a challenge in one-semester courses.

Assigning seats and making a Seating Chart.

PAIN POINTS

It is difficult to enforce this practice in senior classes. Types of seats and schooling and number of students affect its implementation.

OPPORTUNITIES

Assocation through location is an important technique for memorisation. Memory palaces can be used as substitutes where teachers use virtual environments to place their students.

Miscellaneous (Using name cards or display tents, Going through attendance records timely.)

PAIN POINTS

Enforcing this practice across different streams and schooling stages and cultures is challenging. Even though ‘matching faces to names’ is achieved, teachers do not form complete mental images of students, which is the ultimate goal.

‘Matching faces to names’ is only just the face of a bigger hidden need.

While most teachers agreed on the reasons for the need to remember students’ names, many were of the opinion that just achieving the same doesn’t help a lot.

“Remembering names of students helps: agreed, but it is superficial. If you are trying to know all your students, why not go all the way. Names don’t say anything about a student...I prefer to keep a list of the interests, hobbies, weakness of students. Apart from allowing me to know my students, background information helps me to shape my teaching material so that students can take maximum advantage”.

-A high school teacher on reddit.

On further inquiry, I found out a fair number of teachers kept a tab of their students’ interests, hobbies, likings etc. at least in memory if not on paper. Though student interaction decreased with increase in the size of classes and stage of schooling, several teachers even in universities and grad-schools kept notes of student information as it helped them to have better conversations with students in class or even over lunch and outside schools.

Designingthe experience

Understanding how memory works:

Understanding how remembering and memorisation works was difficult. What was more difficult was that I always had to remind myself of the concepts while designing the experience. So I decided to draw out comics on paper (which I later converted into digital sketches for fun) that explained the ideas in a way that was easier to understand whenever I needed referencing.

Still too much, can you make it smaller? , Summing up:

Consolidation

Consolidation is the process by which information moves from short-term memory space to long-term memory space in the brain. Revisiting a memory timely helps consolidate it, thereby making it possible to remember it even a long time after the time of its occurrence.

CONSEQUENCE

Enabling controlled practice of matching face to names can help teachers memorise thoroughly. The practice can be spaced to memorise all names.

Methods for better encoding

Information can sometimes be encoded into long-term memory space with relatively lesser practice by using specific methods. Forming more and more associations and using encoding techniques that enable easier recall helps in storing certain information in long-term memory space. Methods like grouping similar information (chunking), absurd association, mnemonics help.

CONSEQUENCE

Using associations such as ‘one-line description’ or ‘alliterating adjectives’ to names can help teachers memorise them quickly.

Recall

When we encode information into our memory space, we encode by fragments. The visual part, the auditory part, semantic part etc. of the information are encoded into different parts of the brain and at the same time connected. And more the fragments or connections, the easier it is to recall a memory.

If I just have a new word ‘groo’, it is difficult to remember it at a later period only by itself. Instead, if I have an image of ‘groo’ and meaning of ‘groo’, I can recall it easily. The brain latches on to different fragments and connections and fires up the particular memory.

Additionally, active recall is a better way to remember things than recognising as it strengthens the connections between fragments.

CONSEQUENCE

Having more information (unique hobbies, quirky characteristics) than just names and faces can help teachers form complete mental images of students. Using active recall as a method for consolidation.

Now that I had outlined how the app would help teachers in memorisation, I moved to design the complete solution.

Building Google Classbook

Oops am I stuck? , First hurdle and getting around it.

The app needed data in the form of names and pictures of students to be operable. While teachers could always input data by themselves, I realised it was not a feasible option. Teachers at the start of a semester are already burdened by paperwork, material preparation and other stuff. A class of 60-80 students would certainly discourage teachers from populating student data. The adoption of the app in such a case becomes low because of the barrier before the goal. It was clear; I had to get around the problem.

At this point, one could ask why not use the data from directories that every school kept. For example, if a school maintains a spreadsheet of student information of every class, we could find a way to covert the spreadsheet into populating the app. A run-of-the-mill inquiry into the functioning of schools revealed that such paperwork was mostly offline and even if they were online, several schools had policies to not directly share student information with individual teachers due to privacy reasons.

I had to find a proper pipeline through which data could be populated automatically. Researching, I found out Google already had a classroom app ‘Google Classroom’. The recent pandemic had made the app immensely popular. The reason partly being it could be used to make announcements, provide material and assignments for a class in a single channel. Teachers sent out a classroom code, and students had to enter the code in their app to be a part of the class on Google Classroom. This junction appeared to be the perfect gateway for automatic data population for teachers.

Consolidation: the hero everyone needs

Teachers know a particular name belongs to a specific student when they visit the app. But the information lies in the short-term memory space of users unless it is consolidated to long-term memory space. It can be achieved by revisiting the information time-to-time.

I tried to research and think about how to implement the above concept. Internet research led me to find out about flashcards and how they are the most effective method to memorise practically anything.Though I had preliminary ideas on different consolidation methods (choose correct names from multiple option, see and type etc), I decided to use flashcard technique because of below reasons:

When we look at the front side of a flashcard and think of the answer, we are engaging a mental faculty :active recall. In other words, we are attempting to remember the name from scratch rather than simply staring at the name or choosing from several names in a multiple choice quiz. Active recall has been proven to create stronger neuron connections for that memory trace.

NO CLASSROOM? NO PROBLEM

Practise Tab

The last tab in the bottom navigation bar leads to the practise area where teachers can use flashcards to practise matching face to names. Additionally, Voice-based pronounciation helps in pronouncing difficult names as well as acts as an additional cue for memorisation. An user can casually practice by scrolling through cards on the tab or use flashcard test to test out memory

Practising memorisation with Flashcard test

Flashcard test allows teachers to work on their memory. After picking a goal, the user sees a particular face on a flashcard. This initiates recall as the user tries to type out the name. Additionally, he can use hints, which comprise the hobbies, notes and other information about it, to help himself. If he cannot guess still, he can reveal the answer. Recalling the correct name to a particular face continuously determines if it has been memorised to a reasonable level.

But what decides the order in which flashcards appear?

According to Wikipedia, ‘Spaced repetition is an evidence-based learning technique that is usually performed with flashcards. Newly introduced and more difficult flashcards are shown more frequently, while older and less difficult flashcards are shown less frequently to exploit the psychological spacing effect. The use of spaced repetition has been proven to increase the rate of learning.’

Having practice memorisation through flashcards seems to do the job. The difficulty comes in when teachers have 80-120 or more students in their class. Going through each card in every practice session is hard to achieve in reality. While we can randomise card order to reduce the limitation, it won’t help achieve memorisation effectively. This led me to look for methods that memory practitioners use to practice large deck for flashcards.

When we look at the front side of a flashcard and think of the answer, we are engaging a mental faculty :active recall. In other words, we are attempting to remember the name from scratch rather than simply staring at the name or choosing from several names in a multiple choice quiz. Active recall has been proven to create stronger neuron connections for that memory trace.

In one such application of spacing effect, Leitner system, correctly answered cards are advanced to the next, less frequent box, while incorrectly answered cards return to the first box for more aggressive review and repetition.

I  decided to loosely base my algorithm of card appearance on the Leitner System.

All the workings and functionalities of the app were drawn out till this point.  However, while discussing my ideas with some of my teacher relatives, I found out some shortcomings.

  • Correctly answered cards without the use of hints move to the second level. Similarly cards at the second level move to the third level and so on upon answering correctly.
  • Correctly answered cards without the use of hints move to the second level. Similarly cards at the second level move to the third level and so on upon answering correctly.
  • Cards, when answered by using hints, remain at the same level. While cards that use reveal or are answered wrong, go down by one level unless they are on the base level in which case they don’t move.
  • If a card reaches level 5 and answered correctly will henceforth attain a good memorisation level and deemed as memorised.

Oops am I stuck? Part 2

All the workings and functionalities of the app were drawn out till this point.  However, while discussing my ideas with some of my teacher relatives, I found out some shortcomings.

While there were limited functions on the app, they were co-related. For example, practising flashcards without filling up all student data won’t help much. Similarly, just adding students without practising memorisation won’t help achieve the goal too. At the same time directly jumping to memorisation would not work, if one has not gone through the information even once. Because the way it works is information has to be in the short term memory before it can be memorised.

In short, there is a specific sequence that users have to follow to achieve the goal in the least possible time.

Since the app requires users to open it periodically, they had to be given a cue to do so. Every level-based game or app has a way of reminding users of their current level and motivating them to level up.

A percentage of teachers recognise themselves as averse to technology. In such a case, apps have to provide a sense of direction to users and tell them what to do at every stage with adequate feedback.

All of the above cases meant that I had to find a way to solve the problem

Zeigarnik Effect: Why is it so hard to leave things incomplete

Zeigarnik effect theory states that a task in progress creates task-specific tension. This tension is relieved when the task is completed, but if the task is interrupted, it stays. That tension makes relevant information more accessible and more easily remembered. I looked at apps similar to my use case and had some findings.

Duolingo tempts users to come back to the app to achieve goals by using the Zeigarnik effect. Any particular goal is divided into different levels or tasks, and the user is informed of the completion level of each lesson. Similar techniques are also employed by Twitter and Quora to add value to simple tasks and motivate users to go deep into the process of the app.

Also, they act as an incentive to ask users to come back to the app often and give them direction to obtain maximum benefits from the app. These findings perfectly suited my use case, and I moved forward to apply them in the app.

Tasks Tab

Within every class on Google Classbook, there is a set of preset tasks that users have to complete. At the top, also users are informed of their current progress, the number of goals names that they have memorised and percentage of tasks that they have completed.

While some tasks were elementary such as completing all details of a student profile and adding notes, other tasks appeared as users made progress with memorisation.

I did not think of the intricacies and implications of every task due to time constraints. Some tasks such as ‘viewing every profile’ (an important process in memorisation) were deemed as important, but there was no exact way to track the progress.
In the future, I will explore if a user can tick off task by himself in which progress tracking becomes redundant.

Reasoning behind ‘Google Classbook’

08

The name  of the app had to be catchy and also, ideally, feel like something that would fit in the Google ecosystem.

‘Google Classbook’ seemed to strike a perfect balance. Naming it so makes it a part of a sub ecosystem ‘Google Classroom’. This particularly emphasises the connection between two apps. The visual language and the logo were also designed so as to unify the visual identity and in fact, create a strong brand presence of ‘Classroom’ for a possible chain of ed-tech apps under the ‘Classroom’ brand in the future.

At the same time, the name also reemphasises the goal the app was created for: To help teachers create a personalised student information system that helps them build better relations with students apart from helping them memorising names.

Staring back and Pushing Forward

Some things that I did not explore:

There were several areas of the experience and ideas that I did not explore due to the limited timeframe

Onboarding

Onboarding is naturally important in such a case. Can a high-school professor be taught about the functioning of all features in the onboarding itself? Coach marks :too intrusive  or might add confusion?

Session History

Accessing previous results to analyse performance is an essential part of every practice.
Enabling users to understand which time provides maximum memorisation efficiency helps achieve their goal faster.

Task flow

Testing how class environment can impact the task flow. Adding imformation picked up during class to the app might interrupt the teacher. Testing a better task-flow to minimise interruption.

Notifications

Reminding users to use flashcard test. Can Google Calendar be integrated with the app? Teachers use Calendar to plan their classes. Notifying users to practise memorisation just before a class increases the efficiency of memorisation.

Assumptions

There were several assumptions that I undertook while building the app. Future steps would be to validate these assumptions.

Some major assumptions include teachers, value understanding their students, and they are willing to put in efforts.
Do the teachers want this, or is it worthwhile to invest time in building the product?
